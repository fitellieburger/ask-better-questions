# Ask Better Questions — Teacher Guide
**Grades 6–12 | Media Literacy Implementation Tool**

---

## What This Tool Does

Ask Better Questions (ABQ) is a free browser tool that reads any online article and generates three types of prompts to deepen student engagement with the text:

- **Ask Better Questions** — 3 probing questions about what the article claims and whether those claims hold up
- **Be More Curious** — 3 questions that push students toward context, background, and what's left out
- **Get Better Answers** — 3 search cues that help students know what to look up next

Students paste a URL or article text, and the tool responds in seconds. No account required. No student data collected or stored.

Each output includes a **why** — a brief explanation of why that question matters for this particular article. This is the part worth discussing in class.

## Setup

ABQ works two ways — use whichever fits your school's tech environment:

**Option 1 — Chrome Extension (recommended)**
1. Open Chrome and go to the Chrome Web Store
2. Search "Ask Better Questions" and click **Add to Chrome**
3. Navigate to any article and click the ABQ icon in the toolbar
4. The panel opens automatically — no login, no setup

**Option 2 — Web App**
1. Go to [ask-better-questions.onrender.com](https://ask-better-questions.onrender.com)
2. Paste any article URL or copy-paste article text
3. Results appear in the same browser tab

**FERPA note:** ABQ does not collect, store, or transmit student data. No accounts are created. Article text is processed to generate questions and immediately discarded. Safe for classroom use.

## Three Classroom Scenarios

**1. Before reading — Set a reading purpose**
Before assigning an article, run it through ABQ and share one question from each mode on the board. Ask students: *Which of these do you think the article will answer? Which won't it answer?* Students read with a specific lens, not just for comprehension.

**2. During reading — Pause and interrogate**
Stop students halfway through an article. Run ABQ on the full text. Have students discuss: *Has the article addressed any of these questions yet? Does knowing these questions change what you're paying attention to?* Works especially well with opinion pieces and news analysis.

**3. After reading — Know what to search next**
Use "Get Better Answers" mode after students finish an article. The tool generates search cues based on what's missing or unverified in the text. Students use those cues for a 10-minute follow-up search and report back what they found. Builds the habit of treating one article as a starting point, not a conclusion.

## Discussion Questions

**Middle School (Grades 6–8)**
1. What did this article want you to believe? How do you know?
2. Which question from ABQ surprised you most — why?
3. What information would you need to fully trust this article?
4. Who wrote this, and what might they want you to think?
5. What would someone who disagreed with this article say?

**High School (Grades 9–12)**
1. What claims does this article make that it doesn't support with evidence?
2. Where does the article tell you what to think versus giving you information to decide?
3. What perspective or voice is absent from this piece?
4. If you followed the "Get Better Answers" search cues, how did your view of the article change?
5. What's the difference between a question the article raises and a question the article avoids?
6. How would this article read differently if written by someone with the opposite point of view?

## Common Pitfalls

- **ABQ doesn't fact-check.** It identifies claims worth verifying — students still have to do the verification. Frame it as a starting point, not a verdict.
- **ABQ doesn't summarize.** If students use it to avoid reading, the questions won't make sense without the article. Reading comes first.
- **Not every question will land for every article.** Some articles are thin and the tool will say so. That's a useful data point — discuss why.

## Standards Alignment

| NAMLE Core Principle | How ABQ addresses it |
|---|---|
| **Access** | Free, no account, works on any article students already encounter |
| **Analyze** | Surfaces structure, sourcing, and framing questions for every text |

| **Act** | "Get Better Answers" mode turns passive reading into active information-seeking |
